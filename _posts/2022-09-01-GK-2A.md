<!-- Intro Section -->
<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
Sri Lanka sits in a very interesting position geographically for satellite reception. Several geostationary weather satellites are visible from here, and with the right equipment it is possible to receive their direct broadcast data transmissions. Over time I plan to explore and document reception of some of these satellites in future posts.
</p>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
However, <b style="color:#00ffff;">GEO-KOMPSAT-2A (GK-2A)</b> holds a special place for me. Unlike many other geostationary weather satellites that require large antennas or more specialized hardware, GK-2A can be received using relatively modest equipment — a small satellite dish, an SDR receiver, and a low-noise amplifier. This makes it one of the most accessible geostationary weather satellites for hobbyists and radio enthusiasts in this region.
</p>

<!-- Info Panel -->
<div style="background:rgba(0,255,255,0.05); border-left:3px solid #00ffff; padding:1.5rem; margin:1.5rem 0; border-radius:0 8px 8px 0;">
  <h3 style="margin-top:0; color:#00ffff; font-size:1.3rem;">📡 Satellite Overview</h3>
  <p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:0;">
  Another reason GK-2A stands out is that it broadcasts far more than just the familiar full-disk Earth imagery. In addition to high quality satellite images, the LRIT service distributes a wide variety of meteorological and oceanographic products including weather charts, regional and global wave models, sea surface temperature maps, surface pressure maps, and forecast data. When received continuously, this stream of information effectively provides a near real-time view of weather systems across a large portion of the Asia-Pacific region.
  </p>
</div>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
GK-2A is a South Korean geostationary meteorological satellite positioned at <b style="color:#00ffff;">128.2°E</b>. It continuously transmits meteorological imagery and environmental data over L-band at <b style="color:#00ffff;">1692.14 MHz</b> using the <b style="color:#00ffff;">LRIT (Low Rate Information Transmission)</b> service. The satellite has a large coverage footprint spanning <b style="color:#ffa500;">Asia, Australia, New Zealand, and eastern Russia</b>, which makes it easily receivable from Sri Lanka.
</p>

<!-- Note Box -->
<div style="background:rgba(0,255,255,0.05); border-left:4px solid #00ffff; padding:1rem 1.5rem; margin:1.5rem 0; border-radius:0 8px 8px 0;">
  <p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin:0;">
  <strong style="color:#00ffff;">💡 Note:</strong> The satellite also broadcasts a higher bandwidth <b>HRIT (High Rate Information Transmission)</b> service at <b style="color:#00ffff;">1695.4 MHz</b>. While HRIT carries higher resolution imagery, it requires a larger antenna and tighter link margins to receive reliably.
  </p>
</div>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
From Sri Lanka the satellite appears relatively low on the eastern horizon, but still high enough to maintain a stable link margin when using a properly aligned dish. With careful alignment and a low-noise receiving chain, reliable reception is possible even with relatively small antennas.
</p>

<!-- RF Chain Section -->
<h2 style="color:#00ffff; border-bottom:2px solid rgba(0,255,255,0.3); padding-bottom:0.5rem; margin-top:2rem;">📡 RF Chain & Signal Path</h2>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
The overall reception chain is relatively simple and can be implemented using commonly available SDR hardware. The dish collects the L-band signal from the satellite, which is amplified by the low-noise amplifier before being passed to the SDR receiver for demodulation and decoding on a Raspberry Pi.
</p>

<!-- Software Section -->
<h2 style="color:#00ffff; border-bottom:2px solid rgba(0,255,255,0.3); padding-bottom:0.5rem; margin-top:2rem;">💻 goesrecv & xrit-rx</h2>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
To decode the LRIT broadcast I installed 
<a href="https://github.com/sam210723/goestools" style="color:#00ffff; text-decoration:none;">goesrecv</a> and 
<a href="https://github.com/sam210723/xrit-rx" style="color:#00ffff; text-decoration:none;">xrit-rx</a> on a Raspberry Pi 4.
</p>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
After configuring the <code style="background:rgba(0,0,0,0.3); padding:0.2rem 0.4rem; border-radius:4px; color:#ffa500;">goesrecv.conf</code> file for the RTL-SDR V3, I downloaded the file 
<code style="background:rgba(0,0,0,0.3); padding:0.2rem 0.4rem; border-radius:4px; color:#ffa500;">EncryptionKeyMessage_001F2904C905.bin</code> from the 
<a href="http://nmsc.kma.go.kr/enhome/html/base/cmm/selectPage.do?page=satellite.coms.operOverview" style="color:#00ffff; text-decoration:none;">NMSC website</a>. 
This file contains the decryption key required for the LRIT broadcast.
</p>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
Once decrypted and configured as <code style="background:rgba(0,0,0,0.3); padding:0.2rem 0.4rem; border-radius:4px; color:#ffa500;">EncryptionKeyMessage.bin</code> within xrit-rx, the reception pipeline was started using:
</p>

<!-- Code Block -->
<div style="background:#1a1a2e; border-radius:8px; overflow:hidden; margin:1rem 0;">
  <pre style="margin:0; padding:1rem; color:#00ffff; font-family:'Courier New', monospace; font-size:0.9rem;">goesrecv -v -i 1 -c goesrecv.conf</pre>
</div>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
In a second terminal the xrit-rx decoder was started with:
</p>

<div style="background:#1a1a2e; border-radius:8px; overflow:hidden; margin:1rem 0;">
  <pre style="margin:0; padding:1rem; color:#00ffff; font-family:'Courier New', monospace; font-size:0.9rem;">python3 xrit-rx.py</pre>
</div>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">

<a href="https://github.com/nullpainter/sanchez" style="color:#00ffff; text-decoration:none;">Sanchez</a> and the

</p>

<p style="text-align:justify; line-height:1.7; margin-bottom:1.5rem;">
These tools allow additional processing such as applying false color palettes, adding geographic overlays and underlays, and embedding timestamps. Since GK-2A transmits a <b style="color:#00ffff;">full disk image every 10 minutes</b>, a total of <b style="color:#ffa500;">144 images per day</b> can be collected and combined into smooth time-lapse animations.
</p>
